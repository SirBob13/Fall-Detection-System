## ABSTRACT
Falls represent a major health and safety risk for older adults and other vulnerable individuals, particularly when timely assistance is unavailable. Delayed fall detection may lead to severe injuries, prolonged recovery, and serious medical complications. ## CHAPTER (2): LITERATURE REVIEW & BACKGROUND

This chapter reviews the theoretical and technical background relevant to the proposed system. ### 2.1 Overview of Fall Incidents
Falls are among the most serious incidents affecting older adults and other vulnerable individuals who may experience reduced balance, slower reflexes, muscle weakness, or health conditions that increase the likelihood of sudden collapse. A fall can occur unexpectedly during ordinary daily activities such as walking, standing, turning, or sitting down. Although some falls result in minor injuries, many lead to fractures, head trauma, hospitalization, reduced mobility, and long-term psychological effects such as fear of movement and loss of independence.

The danger of falls is not limited to the incident itself, but also to the delay in response that may follow. When a fall is not detected immediately, the affected individual may remain without assistance for a critical period of time. This delay can worsen medical outcomes and increase the risk of severe complications. For this reason, fall detection has become an important research area in healthcare technology, especially within the broader context of remote monitoring and smart assistive systems.

Traditional methods of fall observation, such as direct caregiver supervision or periodic physical checking, are often impractical for continuous use. Accordingly, researchers have focused on technological solutions that enable automatic fall recognition with minimal user intervention. Among these solutions, wearable systems have received considerable attention because they offer continuous monitoring in daily life while avoiding many of the limitations associated with environmental sensing systems.

### 2.2 Wearable Devices in Healthcare Monitoring
Wearable devices have become an essential component of modern healthcare monitoring due to their ability to collect physiological and movement-related data continuously in real time. Advances in embedded systems, low-power microcontrollers, miniaturized sensors, and wireless communication technologies have made wearable healthcare devices increasingly practical, affordable, and accessible.

Examples of wearable health technologies include smartwatches, fitness bands, ECG patches, pulse oximetry devices, and fall-detection wearables. These systems are particularly valuable because they remain attached to the user and therefore operate independently of a fixed physical location. As a result, monitoring can continue whether the user is indoors, outdoors, alone, or moving between environments.

In the context of fall detection, wearable devices offer several important advantages. They provide direct access to body motion signals, enable continuous monitoring without relying on cameras, and preserve privacy by collecting sensor measurements rather than images or video. In addition, wearable devices can be integrated with cloud services, mobile applications, and alert systems, making them suitable not only for detection but also for response and follow-up. For these reasons, wearable monitoring has become one of the most promising directions for intelligent fall detection systems.

### 2.3 Motion Sensors for Activity and Fall Detection
The effectiveness of a wearable fall detection system depends heavily on the quality and relevance of the sensor data it collects. Since falls are motion-related events, inertial sensors are among the most suitable sensing technologies for this task. These sensors capture changes in movement, orientation, and angular motion, allowing the system to distinguish abnormal patterns from ordinary daily activities.

The most commonly used motion sensors in wearable fall detection are the accelerometer and gyroscope. When combined within an inertial measurement unit, they provide complementary information about body movement and posture dynamics. This makes them highly effective for recognizing both sudden impacts and changes in orientation that typically occur during a fall.

#### 2.3.1 Accelerometer
An accelerometer measures linear acceleration along three axes, usually denoted as X, Y, and Z. It is capable of detecting sudden movement changes, impact intensity, and shifts in orientation relative to gravity. In fall detection applications, the accelerometer is particularly useful for identifying rapid downward motion, free-fall-like phases, and strong impact events.

The accelerometer is often the first sensor used in basic fall detection systems because of its simplicity and low computational requirements. However, acceleration signals alone may not always be sufficient to distinguish a genuine fall from other high-intensity actions such as quickly sitting down, jumping, or abrupt body movements. Therefore, although the accelerometer is essential, it is typically more effective when used in combination with other sensors.

#### 2.3.2 Gyroscope
A gyroscope measures angular velocity, which reflects the rate of rotational movement around the three spatial axes. In a fall event, body posture often changes rapidly, and these rotational changes may provide important evidence that is not fully captured by acceleration data alone.

Gyroscope measurements are especially useful for identifying sudden tilting, twisting, or loss of postural stability. When used together with accelerometer data, the gyroscope improves the system's ability to differentiate between dangerous falls and normal daily movements. This complementary role makes it a key component in more advanced wearable fall detection systems.

#### 2.3.3 Sensor Fusion
Sensor fusion refers to the process of combining measurements from multiple sensors in order to produce a more reliable representation of motion. In wearable fall detection systems, sensor fusion is commonly applied to accelerometer and gyroscope data to improve accuracy, reduce noise, and capture complex movement patterns more effectively.

By integrating linear and rotational motion information, sensor fusion provides a richer and more stable description of the user's activity. This improves the system's ability to identify fall-related behavior while reducing false alarms caused by isolated signal fluctuations. Sensor fusion can be performed using classical filtering techniques, such as complementary or Kalman filtering, or through machine learning and deep learning methods that learn the relationship between multiple signal streams directly from data.

### 2.4 Artificial Intelligence Techniques for Fall Detection
Artificial intelligence has become increasingly important in fall detection because falls are complex temporal events that may not be captured accurately through fixed thresholds alone. AI models are capable of learning hidden patterns, temporal dependencies, and subtle variations in motion data, making them highly suitable for distinguishing fall events from normal activities of daily living.

In wearable systems, AI-based methods are especially valuable because motion signals are sequential by nature. The analysis of such data requires models that can capture not only the magnitude of change at a single moment, but also the order and evolution of movements over time. For this reason, time-series classification has become a central task in intelligent fall detection research.

#### 2.4.1 Machine Learning for Time-Series Classification
Earlier fall detection approaches often relied on conventional machine learning models such as Support Vector Machines, Decision Trees, Random Forests, and Logistic Regression. These methods typically operate on manually engineered features extracted from raw sensor signals. Examples of such features include mean acceleration, variance, signal magnitude, tilt angle, peak intensity, and angular displacement.

Conventional machine learning methods can perform well when the feature engineering process is carefully designed. However, their success often depends heavily on expert knowledge, task-specific signal processing, and the quality of handcrafted features. As a result, their performance may decrease when dealing with complex motion patterns, noisy sensor streams, or large variations between users.

Although machine learning remains useful in many wearable applications, modern fall detection research increasingly favors deep learning approaches because they reduce the need for manual feature design and can learn more expressive representations directly from raw or minimally processed sensor data.

#### 2.4.2 Deep Learning for Motion Analysis
Deep learning models are well suited to fall detection because they can learn temporal and nonlinear patterns directly from sequential sensor data. Among the most widely used architectures for wearable time-series analysis are recurrent neural networks, especially Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) and Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) models.

LSTM networks are designed to capture long-term dependencies in sequential data and are particularly effective when important motion patterns extend across multiple time steps. This makes them appropriate for modeling the progression of a fall event from initial imbalance to impact. GRU networks provide a similar capability with a simpler internal structure and fewer parameters, which often leads to faster training and lower computational complexity. In practice, LSTM models are often preferred when richer temporal memory is needed, whereas GRU models are attractive when computational efficiency is a greater concern.

Attention mechanisms can further improve recurrent models by allowing the network to focus on the most informative parts of the sequence. In fall detection, this is useful because not all moments in a sensor window are equally important. Some segments may represent ordinary movement, while others contain the critical changes associated with loss of balance or impact. By emphasizing these relevant time steps, attention-based models can improve both interpretability and performance.

Because different architectures may capture complementary aspects of the data, ensemble methods are also effective. By combining the predictions of multiple models, ensemble approaches often provide greater stability, improved generalization, and reduced false positives compared with single-model systems.

#### 2.4.3 Why Vision-Based Models Are Not Suitable
Vision-based models, including camera-based activity recognition and object detection systems, have also been explored for fall detection. While these methods may achieve strong performance in controlled environments, they present several limitations that reduce their suitability for the type of system proposed in this project.

First, camera-based monitoring raises significant privacy concerns, particularly in bedrooms, bathrooms, and private home environments. Second, such systems depend on environmental conditions such as lighting, camera angle, occlusion, and field of view. Third, they are location-dependent, meaning that monitoring is limited to spaces where cameras are installed. Finally, vision-based systems generally require greater computational resources and may not be as practical for continuous personal monitoring.

In contrast, wearable sensor-based systems are more portable, privacy-preserving, and suitable for real-world use across different locations. For these reasons, the present project adopts a sensor-driven AI approach rather than a vision-based one.

### 2.5 Related Work
A wide range of fall detection systems has been proposed in the literature, and these systems can generally be grouped into three major categories: threshold-based methods, conventional machine learning approaches, and deep learning approaches.

Threshold-based methods are among the earliest and simplest techniques. They usually depend on detecting sudden acceleration peaks, large orientation changes, or combinations of manually selected signal conditions. Although such methods are computationally lightweight and suitable for embedded devices, they often suffer from limited robustness and high false-positive rates.

Conventional machine learning approaches improved upon threshold-based systems by using extracted features and statistical classification models. These approaches demonstrated better flexibility and higher accuracy in many cases, but still relied heavily on feature engineering and often struggled with complex temporal behavior.

More recent work has increasingly focused on deep learning models, particularly recurrent networks and hybrid architectures, for direct analysis of inertial sensor data. These models have shown strong potential in distinguishing falls from normal daily activities and in capturing the temporal evolution of motion sequences more effectively than traditional methods. In addition, several studies have emphasized the benefits of combining accelerometer and gyroscope data, applying attention mechanisms, and using ensemble strategies to improve reliability and reduce false positives.

Despite these advances, many existing solutions remain limited in one or more aspects, such as dependence on a single sensor, insufficient real-time integration, lack of complete mobile and backend support, or reduced applicability in privacy-sensitive environments. This motivates the design of more comprehensive and practical fall detection platforms.

### 2.6 Literature Gap and Research Motivation
Although the literature demonstrates substantial progress in automatic fall detection, several research and implementation gaps remain. One common issue is the difficulty of distinguishing actual falls from normal but abrupt daily movements, which can lead to false alarms. Another challenge is the variability of user behavior, body movement patterns, and sensor placement, all of which can affect model performance.

Many studies focus mainly on model accuracy in offline experiments without addressing complete system integration. In practice, however, fall detection is not only a classification problem; it is also a system-design problem that requires reliable data acquisition, wireless communication, real-time backend processing, user notification, and event management. A model that performs well in isolation may still be insufficient if the surrounding platform is not designed for practical deployment.

In addition, some existing solutions prioritize detection only, without incorporating related health indicators, caregiver communication, or centralized administrative monitoring. This limits their usefulness in real-world healthcare and assistive scenarios.

The motivation for this project, therefore, is to develop a more comprehensive smart fall detection system that combines wearable sensing, AI-based analysis, backend services, mobile alerts, and administrative monitoring in one integrated architecture. The project aims to contribute not only an accurate detection approach, but also a usable end-to-end solution that addresses privacy, portability, and real-time responsiveness in a practical manner.

## CHAPTER (3): METHODOLOGY

This chapter presents the methodology adopted to design, implement, and integrate the proposed fall detection system as a complete end-to-end platform rather than as an isolated AI model. The work was organized around a layered architecture that combines wearable sensing, wireless communication, backend processing, artificial intelligence, mobile interaction, and administrative monitoring. The methodology therefore covers not only how motion and vital data were collected and analyzed, but also how those data were transmitted, stored, verified, visualized, and transformed into actionable alerts for caregivers and administrators.

The proposed system was developed iteratively so that each subsystem could be tested independently before full integration. The hardware prototype was first used to acquire accelerometer, gyroscope, and heart-rate signals. A backend server was then implemented to ingest these measurements, store them in a structured database, and run real-time fall inference using the trained deep learning model. On top of this backend layer, a mobile application was developed for users and caregivers, while a web-based admin dashboard was created to support centralized monitoring, reporting, and operational supervision. This methodological structure ensured that the final solution addressed the practical workflow of fall monitoring in real-world settings.

### 3.1 Overall System Architecture
The proposed system follows a multi-layer architecture composed of four tightly connected layers: the wearable sensing layer, the backend and AI layer, the mobile application layer, and the administrative dashboard layer. This architecture was selected to separate responsibilities clearly while allowing each layer to be extended without redesigning the full system. Instead of embedding all logic inside a single device or application, the project distributes sensing, inference, storage, and user interaction across specialized components.

At the lowest level, the wearable sensing layer is responsible for acquiring raw data from the user. In the implemented prototype, the wearable device is based on an ESP32 microcontroller connected to an MPU6050 inertial measurement unit and a MAX30102 pulse sensor, accessed in firmware through the MAX30105 library. The MPU6050 provides six-dimensional motion data through three-axis accelerometer and three-axis gyroscope measurements, while the MAX30102 is used to estimate heart rate through infrared-based pulse detection. These measurements are collected periodically and formatted into structured JSON payloads that contain device identifiers, user identifiers, motion readings, and, when available, vital values such as heart rate.

The second layer is the backend and AI processing layer. This layer receives device payloads through RESTful API endpoints implemented with FastAPI. Upon arrival, the payload is validated, associated with the correct device and user, stored in the database, and passed into the fall detection pipeline. The AI subsystem loads a trained dual-output model and its corresponding scaler, reconstructs the same engineered features used during training, and performs inference using a rolling time window. The backend then combines the AI result with recent vital information retrieved from the database through a double-verification strategy before creating predictions and alerts. This layer also manages caregiver notification logic, WebSocket-based real-time updates, reporting endpoints, and administrative data access.

The third layer is the mobile application layer. The mobile app serves as the primary interface for end users and caregivers. It allows users to authenticate, manage their profile, connect their wearable device, view alerts, configure emergency contacts, and receive notifications. The application also supports caregiver workflows such as care-link requests, caregiver dashboards, emergency actions, and report viewing. An important methodological choice in this layer was the inclusion of offline handling and BLE-based gateway behavior. When internet connectivity is unavailable, incoming device payloads can be queued locally on the phone and later synchronized in batches once the network is restored.

The fourth layer is the admin dashboard layer. This layer provides a web-based operational view of the whole system. Administrators can monitor users, devices, sensor signals, alerts, and live summary statistics, while also reviewing reports and historical records. The dashboard is connected to the same backend services as the mobile app, but presents the data from a supervisory perspective. This separation between end-user interfaces and administrative interfaces improves usability, simplifies monitoring, and supports future deployment in institutional or caregiving environments.

From a methodological standpoint, the architecture was deliberately designed around modularity and interoperability. Each layer communicates through well-defined interfaces: the wearable sends JSON payloads to the backend, the backend exposes REST and WebSocket interfaces to the mobile app and dashboard, and the AI engine is wrapped as a reusable inference service inside the backend. This made the system easier to debug, test, and extend. It also enabled the project to support both real-time inference and historical analysis, which is important for safety systems that must react immediately while also maintaining logs for later review.

### 3.2 Hardware Design
The hardware subsystem was designed as a wearable prototype capable of collecting continuous motion and selected vital-sign data while remaining compact, low-cost, and suitable for future miniaturization. The main design objective was to create a sensor node that could reliably capture body movement patterns relevant to falls and transmit them to the backend without requiring heavy local computation. For this reason, an ESP32-based design was selected as the central hardware platform.

The ESP32 was chosen because it offers an effective balance between processing capability, connectivity, and power consumption. It supports both Wi-Fi and Bluetooth Low Energy, which was especially useful in this project because the wearable needed more than one communication pathway. Wi-Fi was used for direct backend communication through HTTP requests, while BLE support enabled interaction with the mobile app as a gateway in companion-device scenarios. In addition, the ESP32 provides sufficient digital interfaces and memory for periodic sensor sampling, payload construction, and lightweight signal preprocessing.

To capture motion signals, the design integrates the MPU6050 inertial measurement unit. This sensor combines a three-axis accelerometer and a three-axis gyroscope in a single module. The accelerometer measures linear acceleration along the X, Y, and Z axes, while the gyroscope measures angular velocity around those axes. Together, these two sensing modalities provide a detailed picture of body motion, making them well suited to the identification of fall events. In the firmware, the accelerometer range was configured to `MPU6050_RANGE_8_G`, the gyroscope range to `MPU6050_RANGE_500_DEG`, and the internal bandwidth filter to `MPU6050_BAND_21_HZ`. These settings were used to balance sensitivity and noise reduction while preserving fall-related dynamics.

The project also includes the MAX30102 sensor for optical pulse measurement. Although the primary fall signal in the system comes from the IMU, the MAX30102 provides an additional health-related signal that can support broader monitoring and post-event verification. Its use reflects the project's expansion from simple motion classification toward a richer safety-monitoring platform. In the firmware, the sensor reads infrared values continuously and uses a beat-detection routine to estimate heart rate. To improve signal stability, the implementation includes thresholding, simple smoothing, and a short averaging buffer. If the sensor reading indicates that the finger or skin contact is not valid, the heart-rate value is ignored rather than forced into the payload.

The hardware communication between the ESP32 and sensors is implemented over the I2C bus. In the prototype, `SDA` and `SCL` lines are mapped to GPIO `21` and `22`, respectively. This arrangement simplifies wiring and allows both the IMU and the pulse sensor to share the same bus. During initialization, the firmware starts the I2C interface, configures the selected clock settings, and attempts to initialize each sensor independently. This stepwise initialization is important because it allows the system to continue partially even if one sensor becomes unavailable. For example, if the MAX30102 fails during startup, motion sensing can still continue, and the system can operate without vital data.

The wearable design also reflects practical engineering trade-offs. Running AI inference directly on the microcontroller was intentionally avoided in the current implementation because the project focused on achieving higher model complexity and easier model updates through server-side inference. Offloading inference to the backend reduces the computational burden on the wearable and enables model retraining and redeployment without reflashing the device firmware. At the same time, local data acquisition remains simple and robust. This division of responsibilities was methodologically important because it made it possible to iterate on the AI model independently from the hardware.

From a system-design perspective, the hardware layer was built to support future evolution. The selected ESP32 platform can be extended to transmit through BLE, interact with a companion phone, or integrate additional sensors such as SpO2, temperature, or posture-related modules. Similarly, the modular I2C-based layout allows sensors to be replaced or upgraded with minimal impact on the higher software layers. This makes the prototype appropriate not only for demonstrating the current fall detection workflow, but also as a flexible base for future health-monitoring development.

### 3.3 Firmware and Data Acquisition
The firmware methodology was designed around four main responsibilities: sensor initialization, signal acquisition, lightweight preprocessing, and network transmission. The microcontroller continuously reads motion and pulse-related signals, prepares them in a structured format, and sends them to the backend server through an HTTP-based API. In order to maintain reliable operation, the firmware also includes serial logging, connection checks, and conditional handling for unavailable sensors.

At startup, the firmware initializes the serial interface at `115200` baud to support debugging and runtime observation. It then configures the I2C bus and attempts to initialize the MPU6050 and MAX30102 sensors. In one of the main prototype firmware revisions, the initialization logic was organized into dedicated routines such as `setupMpu6050()` and `setupMax30102()` so that failures could be isolated and diagnosed more easily. In that revision, the IMU configuration explicitly set accelerometer and gyroscope ranges together with filter bandwidth, while the MAX30102 setup configured pulse-sensor parameters and marked the sensor as ready only if initialization succeeded. In the simpler HTTP-streaming revision, the same sensors are initialized directly inside `setup()` while preserving the same overall sensing workflow.

Once the sensors are initialized, the device connects to the Wi-Fi network. The firmware repeatedly checks the connection status until the device is successfully associated with the configured access point. In later refinements, the Wi-Fi logic was improved to avoid unnecessary reconnection attempts and to keep the main loop lightweight. This is important in a real wearable environment because repeated connection attempts can increase latency, clutter serial output, and waste power. After Wi-Fi becomes available, the firmware prints the assigned IP address and begins normal sensing operation.

The motion acquisition stage reads accelerometer and gyroscope values from the MPU6050 at fixed intervals. The sensor library returns acceleration in meters per second squared and angular velocity in radians per second. In the current HTTP-streaming prototype, gyroscope values are explicitly converted to degrees per second before transmission, while acceleration values are transmitted directly from the sensor library output. This representation remains consistent enough for the backend pipeline, which reconstructs its own engineered features during inference.

The pulse acquisition stage is handled separately through an update routine for heart-rate estimation. The firmware reads the infrared value from the MAX30102 and uses it as the primary signal for beat detection. Because optical pulse data can be noisy and contact-sensitive, the code applies a threshold to determine whether the sensor is receiving a valid signal. If the infrared value falls below the threshold, the current heart-rate estimate is reset rather than treated as a true physiological reading. When beat peaks are detected, the time difference between consecutive beats is used to calculate beats per minute, and a short rolling average is maintained to smooth the output. This design reduces false measurements caused by unstable contact or transient noise.

After sampling, the firmware builds a JSON payload for transmission. The payload contains the `device_id`, `user_id`, and a nested `motion` object including `acc_x`, `acc_y`, `acc_z`, `gyro_x`, `gyro_y`, and `gyro_z`. If valid heart-rate data are available, a nested `vitals` object is added as well. This schema is consistent with the backend `DeviceIngestPayload` structure, which allows both motion and vital information to be processed through a unified ingest route. By using a structured JSON schema instead of flat text, the firmware makes the data easier to validate, extend, and reuse across mobile and backend interfaces.

The transmission methodology uses periodic HTTP POST requests to the backend endpoint `/api/v1/device-data`. In the implemented firmware revisions, payload transmission occurs at approximately one- to two-second intervals depending on the prototype version. This interval-based strategy was chosen for simplicity and reliability during prototype development. Each transmission includes the most recent sensor values rather than waiting for a very large local buffer to fill. This improves responsiveness and helps the backend build its own rolling window for inference. The firmware also prints the outgoing payload to the serial monitor, which was especially useful during debugging to confirm that numerical values were being read, converted, and transmitted correctly.

A further methodological aspect of the firmware is its progressive fault tolerance. The system does not assume that all sensors are always available. If the pulse sensor fails, the wearable can continue sending motion data only. If Wi-Fi connectivity is lost, the firmware avoids crashing and can resume communication once the network is restored. In the broader system, BLE monitoring through the mobile app acts as an additional gateway path. This flexibility was important because prototype testing often involved unstable connectivity, variable sensor contact, and changing environmental conditions. Designing the firmware around graceful degradation therefore improved the robustness of the full platform.

Finally, the firmware was designed to be intentionally lightweight. It performs only the minimum preprocessing required for consistent transmission and leaves feature engineering and fall classification to the backend. This decision simplifies embedded code maintenance and ensures that the wearable remains primarily a data-acquisition node. It also keeps the device-side logic aligned with the system's layered architecture, where raw sensing belongs to the hardware layer and intelligent interpretation belongs to the server-side AI layer.

### 3.4 Backend Server Implementation
The backend server was implemented as the central coordination layer of the system. Its main methodological role is to receive sensor measurements from the wearable or mobile gateway, validate and store them, run real-time inference, create alerts when needed, and expose data services to both the mobile application and the administrative dashboard. In practical terms, the backend transforms low-level device signals into structured, actionable, and queryable information.

The server is built with FastAPI, which was selected because it provides high development speed, strong support for typed request validation, automatic API documentation, and good compatibility with asynchronous communication patterns. The application entry point defines the API metadata, enables optional documentation endpoints, configures CORS, initializes the database, loads route groups, and mounts the static admin UI when available. In addition to standard REST routes, the backend includes a WebSocket router to support real-time updates to both users and administrators.

Database persistence is handled through SQLAlchemy models and session management. The backend was designed to work primarily with MySQL during development, while also supporting SQLite fallback for environments where MySQL is unavailable. This dual-database strategy was practical during implementation because it allowed local testing and simplified recovery from environment-specific connection issues. On startup, the backend creates tables if they do not exist and applies safe additive checks for fields such as `users.phone` and `devices.is_archived`. This made the schema more resilient during iterative development.

The database schema was designed around the real data flow of the application. Core tables include `users`, `user_auth`, `user_sessions`, `devices`, `motion_sensor_data`, `vital_sensor_data`, `predictions`, `alerts`, `emergency_contacts`, `emergency_logs`, `care_links`, `care_link_requests`, and `user_push_tokens`. The `motion_sensor_data` table stores raw motion measurements and derived magnitudes, while `vital_sensor_data` stores values such as heart rate, oxygen saturation, temperature, and respiratory rate. The `predictions` table records the output of the AI model, including `fall_now_probability`, `fall_soon_probability`, binary decisions, whether vital verification was performed, and the final confidence score. The `alerts` table stores system-generated warnings and their lifecycle states such as pending, acknowledged, or resolved.

A major methodological feature of the backend is the combined device-ingest route. The endpoint `/api/v1/device-data` accepts a `DeviceIngestPayload` containing `device_id`, `user_id`, optional battery and firmware metadata, and nested `motion` and `vitals` objects. When a payload arrives, the backend first ensures that the device exists and belongs to the correct user. If the device is new, it is registered automatically. If motion data are present, they are passed into the internal motion-processing pipeline. If vital data are present, they are validated and stored separately, and abnormal values may generate their own alerts. This unified ingest design simplifies device communication because the wearable only needs one endpoint regardless of whether it is sending motion only or motion plus vitals.

The internal motion pipeline is especially important. The backend extracts the raw motion sample, associates it with a per-user/per-device buffer key, and passes the sample into the AI prediction service. After obtaining the AI result, the backend stores the raw motion sample in the database and then applies the double-verification module to combine motion evidence with recent vital evidence. A `Prediction` record is then created, and if the final verdict indicates a fall, an `Alert` record may be generated. This alert generation logic includes flood-protection measures to avoid excessive duplicate alerts within short periods. If an alert is created, the backend triggers caregiver notifications and broadcasts the event through WebSockets.

The backend also supports batch ingestion through `/api/v1/device-data/batch`. This route was included to support the mobile app's offline queue. When the phone is temporarily offline, incoming device payloads can be stored locally and later uploaded in groups. The batch endpoint processes each payload using the same internal logic as single-item ingestion, which ensures consistency between real-time and delayed synchronization. This design decision was important because it allowed offline resilience without requiring a separate storage format or inference workflow.

Authentication and session management are handled through a dedicated auth service. User passwords are hashed with `bcrypt`, and successful logins generate both access and refresh tokens. Active sessions are recorded in the `user_sessions` table, enabling server-side session tracking. The backend also supports social login and maintains linked provider records through the `social_accounts` table. For administrative access, a configurable allowlist of admin email addresses is used, and admin-oriented routes are separated under `/api/v1/admin`. These measures created a practical authentication architecture for both end users and system supervisors.

The backend's notification strategy includes both internal and external channels. Internally, real-time updates are sent through WebSocket broadcasts to subscribed mobile clients and admin dashboard sessions. Externally, caregiver notifications can be sent through Expo push notifications and, optionally, SMS via Twilio. Notification tokens are stored per user and per device, allowing push delivery to multiple caregiver endpoints. This methodology turns backend decisions into immediate user-facing actions, which is essential in a fall detection platform where the value of the system depends heavily on timely response.

In addition to core ingest and alert workflows, the backend exposes endpoints for profiles, reports, devices, vitals, predictions, caregiver relationships, and administrative summaries. These routes support mobile screens such as device management, caregiver dashboards, emergency settings, and report views. On the administrative side, separate routes provide overview statistics, detailed user pages, historical motion and vital data, prediction logs, and PDF report export. By centralizing these services in the backend, the system ensures that both the mobile app and dashboard operate from the same source of truth.

Overall, the backend methodology emphasizes structured ingestion, reproducible inference, consistent persistence, and real-time dissemination. It acts as the system's operational core and makes it possible for the wearable, AI model, mobile app, and dashboard to function as one integrated monitoring platform.

### 3.5 AI Model Deployment
The AI component of the project was developed as a dual-output fall prediction system capable of performing both immediate fall detection and short-horizon fall-risk prediction. Methodologically, this layer was designed to bridge the gap between offline model training and online deployment. To achieve this, the same feature engineering and sequence-building logic used during training was reproduced in the inference service with strict consistency.

The training data are stored in a CSV-based IMU dataset and processed through a dedicated Python pipeline. The raw sensor columns corresponding to the wrist accelerometer and wrist angular velocity were extracted from the dataset and converted into numeric values. Missing or inconsistent values were handled using forward filling, backward filling, and zero imputation where necessary. This preprocessing stage ensured that the training process did not break due to malformed values and that sequence generation could proceed without interruption.

Feature engineering was a central part of the AI methodology. Rather than using only the six raw IMU signals, the final model was trained on a richer 16-feature representation. In addition to the raw accelerometer and gyroscope axes, the pipeline computes acceleration magnitude and gyroscope magnitude. It then derives enhanced features including acceleration variance, gyroscope variance, acceleration energy, gyroscope energy, jerk magnitude, mean absolute acceleration value, signal magnitude area, and acceleration standard deviation. These engineered features were selected because they capture both instantaneous motion intensity and short-window temporal behavior, which is crucial for distinguishing falls from ordinary activities.

The target labeling strategy includes two binary outputs. The first output, `fall_now`, indicates whether the current sequence corresponds to a fall event. This label is derived from activity tags associated with known fall classes in the dataset. The second output, `fall_soon`, is designed as an early warning signal. It is computed by scanning a look-ahead horizon and assigning a positive label if a fall is expected to occur within the next fixed number of frames. This dual-output formulation was chosen because the project aimed not only to detect ongoing falls but also to provide earlier risk awareness when possible.

Sequence generation is based on sliding windows of `100` time steps with a step size of `5`. This overlapping-window strategy increases the number of training samples and helps the model learn transitions rather than isolated points. The features are scaled using `StandardScaler`, and the scaler is saved so that exactly the same normalization can be applied during deployment. The dataset is then split into training, validation, and testing subsets in chronological order, after which the model is trained with early stopping, learning-rate reduction, and checkpoint saving.

The final deployed architecture is a dual-output recurrent neural model based primarily on bidirectional LSTM processing with attention. The model begins with batch normalization, followed by two bidirectional LSTM layers with dropout and normalization. A simple attention mechanism is then applied to compute temporal importance weights across the sequence. After global pooling, the shared representation passes through dense layers before branching into two output heads: one for `fall_now` and one for `fall_soon`. This architecture was chosen because recurrent layers capture temporal dependencies effectively, while the attention mechanism helps the network emphasize the most informative parts of a movement sequence.

The model was trained using Adam optimization and a weighted loss strategy. Because fall detection data can be imbalanced, the `fall_now` branch uses a weighted binary cross-entropy formulation to compensate for class distribution effects, while the `fall_soon` branch uses standard binary cross-entropy. The model outputs probabilities rather than hard labels, allowing thresholds to be tuned after training. During evaluation, threshold search was performed to identify operating points with better F1 performance. The final deployment thresholds were then set in the shared configuration as `0.35` for `fall_now` and `0.30` for `fall_soon`.

Deployment inside the backend follows a persistent-service model. The trained Keras model and saved scaler are loaded once and reused across requests. The inference service maintains a rolling raw buffer for each `(user_id, device_id)` stream, ensuring that predictions are based on the recent temporal context rather than on isolated single samples. Every new motion sample is appended to the corresponding deque, and the feature engineering logic reconstructs the same 16-feature representation used during training. If the buffer is shorter than `100` time steps, the system pads the sequence to preserve the required input shape. This warm-up behavior allows the model to begin operating immediately while still converging toward more reliable predictions as the buffer fills.

A particularly important methodological aspect is that the backend does not trust the AI output alone. After the model produces the `fall_now_probability` and `fall_soon_probability`, the result is passed through a double-verification layer that checks whether recent vital signs show abnormal patterns. If both motion evidence and vital evidence support a fall, the final confidence is increased. If motion indicates a fall but vitals remain normal, the event may still be treated as a fall but with lower confidence. This strategy reflects the system-level goal of improving reliability and reducing unnecessary alerts.

The deployed AI service also includes a mock mode for development and API testing. When the model or scaler is unavailable, the backend can still return structured prediction responses. Although mock mode is not part of the actual detection methodology, it was useful during system integration because it allowed frontend and backend development to proceed independently from the final trained model. In the final platform, however, the preferred operating mode is the real dual-output model loaded from the trained artifact.

Overall, the AI deployment methodology was designed to preserve consistency between research and practice. The same feature definitions, window sizes, scaling transformations, and decision thresholds are used in both training and inference. This minimizes deployment drift and ensures that the performance observed during model evaluation is meaningfully reflected in the live system.

### 3.6 Mobile Application Implementation
The mobile application was developed as the main user-facing component of the platform. Its role is not limited to visualizing alerts; it also acts as a management interface for authentication, device pairing, emergency contacts, caregiver interaction, reporting, and real-time monitoring. The app was implemented using Expo and React Native, which allowed a shared mobile codebase while still supporting native integrations such as notifications, biometrics, BLE communication, secure storage, and localization.

The navigation structure was organized around separate flows for authentication and post-login operation. The authentication stack includes login, registration, forgot-password, and reset-password screens. After successful login, users move into the main tab-based interface that includes home, alerts, emergency, and settings functionality. Additional stack screens support personal information, caregiver management, caregiver dashboards, reports, chat, video calls, emergency settings, language settings, device management, and privacy policy access. This multi-stack navigation design was used to keep emergency and settings workflows organized while still giving the user quick access to the most critical functions.

Authentication in the mobile app is handled through a custom auth service that manages local session storage, remote token validation, and smart refresh behavior. The service first performs local expiry checks, then attempts server-side token validation, and finally falls back to refresh-token logic when needed. This design improves user experience by avoiding unnecessary logouts while maintaining session integrity. Session monitoring also runs in the background, allowing the app to refresh tokens proactively and keep the user authenticated across longer periods of use.

The home screen acts as a live summary dashboard for the active user or the currently monitored patient. It loads the latest alerts, connected device state, most recent prediction, and latest vital readings. It also subscribes to WebSocket events through the real-time service so that incoming alerts, device updates, vitals, and prediction records can be reflected immediately in the UI. When a new alert arrives, the app can trigger local notifications and, if the settings permit, automatically initiate emergency workflows. This real-time methodology ensures that the mobile application behaves as a live safety monitor rather than as a purely passive viewer.

Device management was implemented as a dedicated workflow because pairing and maintaining the wearable is central to the system. The app supports Bluetooth scanning, manual device ID entry, connection, disconnection, removal, archive viewing, and restoration. The BLE layer is implemented using `react-native-ble-plx`, while a gateway service listens to a configured BLE characteristic, decodes base64 JSON payloads, parses them into the same ingest schema used by Wi-Fi device traffic, and forwards them into the offline queue. This means the phone can act as a communication bridge when the wearable is connected through BLE rather than sending directly over Wi-Fi.

Offline handling is another important methodological feature of the mobile app. Incoming device payloads are not discarded when network access is unavailable. Instead, they are stored in an `AsyncStorage`-based queue maintained by the offline queue service. When connectivity is restored, the queue is flushed in batches to the backend using the `/device-data/batch` endpoint. This approach supports robustness in real-world use, where both the wearable and the phone may temporarily lose internet access. It also preserves chronological sensor data that would otherwise be lost, improving both safety and historical analysis.

Emergency functionality was implemented as a first-class subsystem rather than as a minor add-on. The emergency service manages user-defined emergency contacts, contact import from the device address book, emergency settings, cooldown logic, and trigger workflows for fall events, manual SOS, abnormal vitals, or inactivity. The service can access device location through Expo Location, use SMS through Expo SMS, and present notifications through Expo Notifications. The inclusion of rate limiting and cooldown logic was methodologically important because emergency actions must be reliable without becoming abusive or repetitive.

The app also supports caregiver interaction through a care-management workflow. Users can create care-link requests using either patient email or phone number, while patients can accept, reject, or cancel such requests. Once a care link exists, the caregiver can switch context inside the app and view data for the monitored patient instead of their own. This feature is reflected in screens such as caregiver dashboards, reports, and communication interfaces. Real-time updates for care workflows are also delivered through WebSocket events, keeping both caregiver and patient views synchronized.

Reporting functionality was implemented to make the system useful beyond immediate alerting. The reports screen fetches summary data for daily, weekly, or monthly intervals and visualizes trends using charts. It also supports switching between self-view and caregiver-monitored users. By integrating reporting into the mobile layer, the system gives users and caregivers access to patterns and summaries rather than isolated incidents only. This is especially valuable for long-term monitoring and care planning.

The mobile app was also designed with accessibility and localization in mind. It includes Arabic and English translations through `i18next`, supports right-to-left layout behavior, and stores the user's language preference locally. Biometric support was implemented using Expo Local Authentication and SecureStore, allowing the app to support faster and more secure re-entry. Together, these choices show that the mobile app was developed not merely as a test client for the backend, but as an operational part of the monitoring ecosystem.

### 3.8 Security and Privacy
Security and privacy were treated as core design considerations throughout the methodology because fall detection systems process personal, behavioral, and potentially health-related data. The first privacy-oriented decision was to avoid cameras entirely. Unlike vision-based monitoring systems, the proposed platform relies on wearable motion and vital data only. This significantly reduces privacy intrusion and makes the system more suitable for personal and indoor use cases where video monitoring would be unacceptable.

At the backend level, authentication and access control are based on hashed passwords, JWT-like access tokens, refresh tokens, and persisted user sessions. Passwords are not stored in plain text; instead, they are hashed using `bcrypt` before insertion into the database. Active sessions are recorded in the `user_sessions` table, which allows the system to track token usage more explicitly than a purely stateless design. Administrative access is further restricted by an allowlist of approved admin emails, and caregiver visibility is controlled through explicit care-link approval workflows rather than unrestricted data sharing.

The mobile application adds another layer of protection through secure local handling of sensitive credentials. Biometric authentication is supported using the device's native secure authentication capabilities, and values intended for secure local storage can be placed in `SecureStore`. This is especially relevant for a health-related application where quick access is important, but unauthorized access to alerts, reports, and personal details must still be limited. The app also separates emergency contacts, session data, and settings into controlled local storage structures rather than exposing them through uncontrolled caching.

Data transmission is structured and authenticated at the application layer. The mobile app sends authenticated requests to the backend using bearer tokens, while the wearable transmits device payloads in a predefined JSON structure. Push tokens are registered explicitly and linked to user accounts so that notification delivery can be controlled. In addition, the backend uses configurable CORS settings, which helps restrict which frontend origins can communicate with the API in production deployments.

Privacy is also reflected in role separation. A normal user sees their own profile and alerts, a caregiver sees data only for patients linked through accepted care requests, and an administrator sees system-wide views. This role-based separation supports the principle of minimum necessary access. The project therefore does not treat monitoring data as globally visible system data, but as data that should be exposed according to relationship and permission context.

Beyond confidentiality, the methodology also addresses reliability and safety. The system includes alert-cooldown and flood-protection logic so that repeated detections do not overwhelm caregivers with duplicate warnings. The mobile app includes offline queueing so that sensor payloads are not lost during temporary connectivity failures. The BLE gateway includes an offline SOS fallback to trigger local emergency behavior when both the device and the phone lack internet access. The backend verifies vital abnormalities against thresholds and recent values, which adds another layer of protection against acting on isolated noisy readings.

At the same time, the project recognizes that prototype implementations require additional hardening before production-scale deployment. Some secrets and configuration values are still prototype-oriented and should be externalized fully into secure environment management and device provisioning mechanisms. Wi-Fi credentials, placeholder keys, and development toggles such as mock prediction mode are acceptable during controlled development but should be replaced by secure provisioning and stricter production policies. Stating this clearly is important in an academic methodology chapter because it distinguishes between a working research prototype and a hardened commercial-grade deployment.

In summary, the system's security and privacy methodology is based on three principles: collect only the data necessary for fall monitoring, restrict access according to user role and verified relationships, and maintain operational safeguards that keep the system reliable even under imperfect real-world conditions. This combination supports the project's broader goal of delivering a practical, privacy-aware, and trustworthy wearable monitoring platform.

### 4.2 Evaluation Methodology and Experimental Setup
The final model evaluation was based on the same sequence construction strategy used during training. Sensor data were represented using 16 engineered features derived from the wrist-mounted IMU signals. The deployed model consumed sliding windows of `100` time steps with a step size of `5`, and all feature values were normalized using the saved `StandardScaler` artifact. This ensured that inference-time behavior remained consistent with the original training configuration.

The model produced two outputs for every inference window: `fall_now` and `fall_soon`. The `fall_now` output represents the probability that the current sequence corresponds to a fall event, while `fall_soon` represents the probability that a fall is likely to occur within the defined look-ahead horizon. In the deployed configuration, threshold values were fixed at `0.35` for `fall_now` and `0.30` for `fall_soon`, as recorded in the project configuration and saved result files. These thresholds were chosen after post-training threshold tuning and were used consistently in both evaluation and live deployment.

The main quantitative metrics used to assess the AI model were accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, and area under the ROC curve (AUC). Confusion-matrix values were also stored so that false positives and false negatives could be analyzed directly. In this project, recall was especially important because a false negative, which corresponds to a missed fall, is significantly more dangerous than an occasional false positive. However, precision and F1-score were also important because excessive false alerts would reduce trust in the system and create unnecessary caregiver burden.

Beyond offline AI evaluation, the backend and system-level validation used the actual integration code. Real-time testing was based on the same API routes used by the wearable and mobile app, especially `/api/v1/device-data`, `/api/v1/device-data/batch`, and `/api/v1/motion`. For streaming and integration checks, the project also included a Python simulator that generated both normal and fall-like motion data and submitted them to the backend repeatedly at short intervals. This allowed the prediction pipeline, alert logic, and event broadcasting system to be exercised under realistic repeated input conditions.

### 4.5 Threshold Selection and Deployment Behavior
One of the important design choices in the final system was to deploy thresholds that differed from the default midpoint value often used in binary classifiers. Instead of using `0.5` uniformly, the final configuration adopted `0.35` for `fall_now` and `0.30` for `fall_soon`. This decision was based on evaluation-driven threshold tuning recorded in the training and testing pipeline, where different thresholds were explored to maximize F1-score while maintaining high recall.

The lower threshold for `fall_soon` is particularly meaningful because early-warning prediction is naturally more uncertain than immediate event recognition. If the threshold were too conservative, the system would fail to surface near-fall behavior early enough to be useful. By selecting a slightly lower threshold, the system becomes more willing to flag increased short-term risk. This is appropriate because the `fall_soon` output is designed as a preventive or warning signal rather than as a final emergency verdict by itself.

In deployment, these threshold values are used inside the backend AI service and apply consistently to both live motion ingestion and evaluation scripts. This methodological consistency reduces the gap between offline research results and real-world backend behavior. It also means that any future threshold changes can be performed centrally and evaluated explicitly rather than implicitly changing behavior across different parts of the codebase.

### 4.6 Real-Time Inference and End-to-End Validation
The AI model was not evaluated only as a static classifier; it was also validated as part of the full streaming system. The backend implementation uses a rolling raw buffer for each `(user_id, device_id)` stream and performs inference on the most recent `100` timesteps. This allows the system to produce real-time predictions as new motion readings arrive rather than waiting for a separate offline processing job. The same feature engineering logic used during training is applied to the live stream before scaling and inference.

To validate this behavior, the project includes a simulator in `Backend/simulator.py`. This simulator tests API availability, creates test data if needed, generates both normal and fall-like motion signals, submits them periodically to the backend, and prints real-time probabilities and alert outcomes. The simulator also sends vital payloads periodically, allowing the double-verification and abnormal-vitals logic to be exercised. This is a useful validation approach because it tests not only raw prediction output, but also the system behavior around alerts, confidence values, and response formatting.

The simulator was designed to run with repeated short-interval submissions, with motion payloads sent approximately every `1.5` seconds and vital payloads injected regularly. This setup is not identical to clinical field deployment, but it provides a practical approximation of continuous streaming and is sufficient for validating buffer accumulation, model warm-up behavior, alert generation, and repeated route execution. Because the simulator reports whether a fall was detected and whether an alert was generated, it also supports qualitative observation of the platform under repeated mixed inputs.

At the API level, live motion processing follows the full backend path. A motion sample is received, associated with a device, passed into `predict_from_sample`, stored in `motion_sensor_data`, converted into a `Prediction` record, and optionally transformed into an `Alert` after double verification. The backend then broadcasts updates through WebSocket channels for `motions`, `predictions`, and `alerts`, allowing both the mobile app and admin dashboard to update immediately. This end-to-end flow confirms that the system functions as an integrated monitoring pipeline rather than as disconnected modules.

The backend was also validated indirectly through the mobile and dashboard clients, both of which depend on many API endpoints beyond health and motion. These include authentication routes, device connection routes, vitals ingestion, alerts retrieval, caregiver workflows, report generation, and administrative summary routes. Because the same backend serves all client layers, successful operation of the app and dashboard provides additional evidence that the API design and persistence layer are functioning coherently.

From a database perspective, the backend also demonstrated structural correctness by creating and using the intended tables for users, devices, motion data, vitals, predictions, and alerts. The fact that the real-time routes could persist these records and then return them through reporting and monitoring routes confirms that the persistence layer was integrated successfully with the inference and UI layers.

### 4.8 Mobile Application Testing
The mobile application was validated through a combination of targeted unit testing and feature-level manual testing. A unit test file is present in `MobileApp/__tests__/auth.service.test.ts`, where the authentication service is tested for basic session lifecycle behavior. The tests verify that the auth service initializes correctly, can save and load session data, and can clear stored session data successfully. Although this test suite is intentionally small, it covers one of the most critical client-side services because session persistence is essential for a stable user experience.

In addition to the unit test, the implemented screens and services indicate that the mobile application underwent functional validation across several workflows. These workflows include login and registration, device connection and disconnection, alert retrieval and acknowledgment, caregiver request creation and handling, report loading, and emergency contact management. The home screen, alerts screen, reports screen, device-management screen, and care-management screen all subscribe to real-time events and update state accordingly, which suggests that the live-data experience was tested as part of system integration rather than as purely static UI rendering.

Offline behavior was also an important test scenario for the mobile layer. The offline queue service was designed to collect device payloads locally and flush them in batches when internet connectivity returned. This mechanism implies validation of at least three states: normal online operation, temporary offline accumulation, and later synchronization. Because this queue is central to reliable field usage, its existence and integration with the device gateway represent an important aspect of application-level robustness.

Emergency-related features were likewise validated conceptually through the implemented service behavior. The mobile app can maintain emergency contacts, request permissions for notifications and contacts, react to incoming alerts, and trigger emergency workflows when automatic SOS is enabled. Since these actions combine device permissions, local storage, navigation, and backend-driven alerting, they represent one of the more complex integration areas of the mobile application. Their successful inclusion strengthens the argument that the project evolved from a simple monitoring demo into a broader safety-support application.

### 4.9 Admin Dashboard Testing
The admin dashboard was validated as the operational monitoring surface of the platform. Its authentication guard checks for an admin token before allowing access to protected pages, which confirms that the interface is not openly accessible as a static page set. Once authenticated, the dashboard connects to the backend WebSocket service and listens for updates to alerts, motions, vitals, predictions, and device states.

The overview page demonstrates system-level testing by loading aggregate counts from the backend and updating them when new events are pushed in real time. This behavior confirms that the dashboard is not dependent on manual page refreshes alone. The user-detail page provides deeper evidence of integration by loading user profile data, linked devices, historical alerts, vitals, motions, and predictions through parallel backend requests. It also supports filtering, status changes, deletion, and PDF export, which means that both read and administrative write operations were validated within the dashboard context.

The reports page adds another layer of validation by aggregating counts over selected periods, displaying trends, and exporting the results as PDF documents. Because the dashboard uses the same backend API and WebSocket channels as the rest of the system, successful dashboard operation confirms the consistency of the server-side reporting and notification infrastructure. It also shows that the backend data model supports not only real-time alerts, but also retrospective operational analytics.

Overall, the dashboard testing shows that administrative supervision is functional at both the snapshot and real-time levels. It can display the current state of the system, react to new events, and drill down into user-specific history. This is especially important for a fall detection platform intended to scale beyond one user and one device.

### 4.10 Discussion
The overall evaluation results indicate that the project was successful in achieving both strong AI performance and coherent end-to-end integration. From the machine-learning perspective, the final dual-output model achieved high accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, and AUC on both `fall_now` and `fall_soon`. These values suggest that the selected feature engineering strategy and recurrent architecture were appropriate for the target problem.

The most important strength of the final model is its high recall. In a fall detection application, missing a true fall is generally more serious than generating a small number of extra alerts. The saved results show that the final system maintained very high recall for both outputs while still controlling false positives reasonably well. This balance suggests that the deployed thresholds were selected appropriately for a safety-oriented system.

At the platform level, the project also demonstrates an important methodological strength: it does not stop at model evaluation. The system supports real-time ingestion, buffer-based inference, double verification with vitals, caregiver notification, offline synchronization, mobile monitoring, and administrative oversight. This makes the project much closer to a deployable smart-health platform than to a standalone AI experiment.

At the same time, the evaluation process also highlights remaining areas for improvement. The automated test coverage is still limited compared with production-grade software expectations, and some integration scenarios were validated mainly through live workflows and simulator behavior rather than through comprehensive formalized end-to-end test suites. In addition, although the saved metrics are strong, wider field validation with more diverse real-world users and motion patterns would be necessary before claiming clinical readiness. These observations do not reduce the value of the project; rather, they clarify the difference between a strong working graduation prototype and a fully hardened operational product.

## CHAPTER (5): CONCLUSION & FUTURE WORK

### 5.1 Conclusion
This project presented the design and implementation of an intelligent wearable fall detection and monitoring system that combines embedded sensing, artificial intelligence, backend services, mobile interaction, and administrative monitoring into one integrated platform. The work addressed the problem of delayed fall detection by proposing a privacy-preserving wearable approach based on IMU data rather than camera-based surveillance. In doing so, the project moved beyond a simple classification model and delivered a complete end-to-end monitoring architecture.

At the hardware level, the system successfully integrated an ESP32 microcontroller with an MPU6050 inertial measurement unit and a MAX30102 pulse sensor. This enabled continuous acquisition of motion and heart-rate-related signals from the user. At the backend level, the platform implemented structured ingestion, database persistence, real-time inference, double verification, alert creation, notification delivery, and reporting. At the client level, the mobile application provided live monitoring, emergency support, caregiver workflows, device management, and offline synchronization, while the admin dashboard delivered a centralized real-time operational view of the system.

From the AI perspective, the final deployed model achieved strong performance on both immediate fall detection and short-horizon fall-risk prediction. The saved evaluation results showed that the dual-output model achieved high recall, high F1-score, and strong AUC values, indicating that the learned representation is effective for the target task. The use of engineered features, sliding-window modeling, recurrent layers, attention, and deployment-aware thresholds all contributed to this outcome.

Taken together, the results demonstrate that the project achieved its primary goal of building a practical and intelligent fall detection system capable of supporting real-time monitoring and response. The final platform illustrates how interdisciplinary engineering can be used to solve a real healthcare problem through coordinated work in embedded systems, machine learning, software engineering, and user-centered application design.

### 5.2 Limitations
Despite the strong outcomes of the project, several limitations remain. The first limitation concerns deployment maturity. While the platform is functional as an integrated prototype, some aspects of the implementation remain development-oriented. For example, certain configuration values, placeholder secrets, and mock-development settings would need to be hardened and externalized before production deployment.

The second limitation concerns dataset and field diversity. Although the final model achieved strong performance on the available data, wearable fall detection is highly sensitive to differences in user movement, sensor placement, body type, health status, and daily behavior. The current model should therefore be viewed as a strong trained prototype rather than a clinically validated universal solution. Broader real-world testing would be necessary to measure generalization across different populations and environments.

A third limitation concerns automated testing depth. The project includes important validation artifacts, API checks, simulator-based streaming tests, and selected unit tests, but it does not yet include a full suite of large-scale end-to-end automated tests across all system layers. This means that although the integrated behavior is demonstrably functional, future work is needed to improve formal software verification and regression protection.

Another limitation is that the current wearable logic remains largely server-dependent for AI inference. This is an acceptable trade-off for the current prototype because it simplifies embedded implementation and makes model updates easier, but it also means that fully autonomous device-side inference is not yet available. In environments with prolonged connectivity loss, this may reduce system responsiveness compared with a future hybrid on-device/cloud approach.

### 5.3 Future Work
There are several promising directions for future improvement. The first is expanding the dataset and performing broader real-world validation with more users, more motion scenarios, and longer-term recordings. This would help measure model robustness under realistic variation and provide stronger evidence of generalization.

The second direction is model enhancement and personalization. Future versions of the system could incorporate adaptive thresholds, user-specific calibration, or online personalization so that detection becomes more closely aligned with the behavior of a specific user rather than relying entirely on a general trained model. Additional experiments could also compare the current architecture with transformer-based or temporal convolutional approaches.

Another important direction is richer multimodal sensing. The current platform already includes heart-rate support, but future iterations could integrate additional signals such as SpO2, skin temperature, respiration patterns, posture estimation, or even environmental context. These signals could improve double verification and allow the system to evolve from fall detection into broader health-risk monitoring.

On the software side, future work should strengthen production readiness. This includes more comprehensive automated testing, secure device provisioning, stronger secrets management, improved deployment automation, and hardened monitoring/logging infrastructure. The mobile application could also be extended with more advanced caregiver collaboration, richer emergency workflows, and deeper analytics in the reports interface.

Finally, a long-term future direction would be hybrid inference. Some lightweight preliminary detection logic could be moved onto the wearable or the mobile gateway, while the backend remains responsible for more advanced verification and historical analysis. This could reduce response time further and improve resilience during connectivity issues.

### 5.4 Final Remark
The proposed system demonstrates that wearable AI-based fall detection can be implemented as a meaningful smart-health platform rather than as an isolated academic model. By combining motion sensing, intelligent inference, backend coordination, mobile alerting, and administrative monitoring, the project delivers a strong graduation prototype with clear practical value. With further validation, hardening, and extension, the system has the potential to grow into a deployable assistive technology platform that improves safety, preserves privacy, and supports timely caregiver intervention for vulnerable users.
